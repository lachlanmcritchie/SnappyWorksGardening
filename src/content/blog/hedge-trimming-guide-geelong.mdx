A well-maintained hedge does more than mark a boundary. It provides privacy, blocks wind, reduces noise, creates habitat for native birds, and adds real structure to your garden's design. Geelong's climate — with its hot summers, mild winters, and persistent coastal winds — suits a wide range of hedging plants, but keeping them in shape requires the right approach at the right time of year.

## Best hedge plants for Geelong gardens

Choosing the right plant for your hedge is the most important decision. The wrong choice means constant maintenance, poor growth, or a hedge that struggles in local conditions.

### Top performers in Geelong's climate

- **Westringia fruticosa (Coastal Rosemary)** — One of the best all-round hedging plants for Geelong. Handles heat, coastal exposure, poor soil, and drought. Grows to around 1.5m and responds beautifully to regular trimming. Grey-green foliage with small white flowers.

- **Lilly Pilly (Syzygium smithii or Acmena smithii)** — A classic hedging choice that creates a dense, glossy screen. Grows quickly to 3-4m if left unclipped. The "Resilience" variety is resistant to psyllid damage, which is important in this region.

- **Murraya paniculata (Orange Jessamine)** — Popular in Geelong for its dense growth, glossy foliage, and fragrant white flowers. Grows well in most soil types. Can be sensitive to heavy frost, so best in sheltered positions.

- **Photinia (Red Robin)** — Loved for its striking red new growth. Makes a strong 2-3m screen hedge. Hardy and fast-growing. Needs regular trimming to maintain density and encourage fresh red foliage.

- **Pittosporum tenuifolium** — A versatile, hardy hedging plant with small wavy-edged leaves. Several varieties including "Silver Sheen" and "Golf Ball" suit different sizes and styles. Handles wind well.

- **Viburnum odoratissimum (Sweet Viburnum)** — A reliable screening hedge that grows quickly to 3-5m. Large glossy leaves create excellent privacy. Produces white flowers in spring.

- **English Box (Buxus sempervirens)** — The traditional choice for formal, low hedges and topiary. Slow-growing and very dense. Prefers some protection from extreme summer heat in Geelong.

### Native options for low-maintenance hedging

- **Callistemon (Bottlebrush)** — Several compact varieties make excellent informal hedges and attract nectar-feeding birds
- **Correa** — Hardy native with bell-shaped flowers, suits low hedges in dry conditions
- **Banksia integrifolia (Coastal Banksia)** — Can be hedged for a larger, natural-looking screen

## When is the best time to trim hedges in Geelong?

Timing varies depending on the plant species and the type of hedge you are maintaining.

### Formal hedges (tight, geometric shapes)

Formal hedges need trimming **two to four times per year** to maintain clean lines:

1. **Early spring (September)** — Shape up after winter growth. This is the main structural trim of the year.
2. **Early summer (December)** — Tidy the flush of spring growth before the heat sets in.
3. **Late summer/early autumn (February-March)** — Clean up summer growth and prepare for the cooler months.
4. **Optional: mid-winter (July)** — A light trim if the hedge has grown unevenly.

### Informal hedges (natural, flowing shapes)

Informal hedges typically need **one to two trims per year**:

1. **After flowering** — For flowering hedges like Westringia, Callistemon, and Murraya, trim after the main flowering period to avoid cutting off next season's buds.
2. **Late autumn** — A light shape-up before winter.

### When NOT to trim

- **During extreme heat** (above 35 degrees) — freshly cut growth is vulnerable to sunburn
- **Just before a forecast frost** — new growth stimulated by trimming can be frost-damaged
- **When birds are nesting** — check your hedge before trimming in spring, as many native birds nest in dense hedges from September to December

## How to trim hedges properly

### Tools you will need

- **Hedge shears** (manual) — best for small, formal hedges and detail work
- **Powered hedge trimmer** (electric or petrol) — essential for larger hedges and tall screens
- **Secateurs** — for cutting individual thick branches that shears cannot handle
- **A string line or guide** — for achieving straight, level tops on formal hedges
- **Safety gear** — gloves, eye protection, and hearing protection for powered trimmers

### Shaping tips

- **Cut the top slightly narrower than the base.** This allows sunlight to reach the lower branches, preventing the common problem of hedges going bare at the bottom.
- **Take small amounts off regularly** rather than cutting back hard occasionally. Most hedging plants respond better to frequent light trimming.
- **Use a string line** stretched between two stakes for long, straight hedges. It makes a visible difference to the finished result.
- **Step back frequently** while trimming to check your work from a distance. It is easy to create dips and bumps when you are working up close.
- **Cut back to the same point** each time or just slightly beyond to maintain the hedge's size. Letting it grow a little larger with each trim leads to an oversized hedge within a few years.

### How to renovate an overgrown hedge

If your hedge has gotten away from you, the renovation approach depends on the species:

- **Hard pruning** (cutting back to bare wood) works for Photinia, Lilly Pilly, Viburnum, and Murraya. They will re-shoot from old wood. Do this in early spring.
- **Gradual reduction** is better for plants like Westringia and Pittosporum that may not re-shoot from old bare stems. Cut back one side hard, let it recover, then do the other side the following year.
- **English Box** can be hard-pruned but recovery is very slow. Patience is required.

## How do you stop a hedge from going bare at the bottom?

This is one of the most common hedge problems, and it usually comes down to two things:

1. **The top is too wide**, shading out the lower growth. Trim the top narrower than the base.
2. **Trimming too infrequently**, which means cutting back into old wood that cannot regenerate. Trim more often, taking less off each time.

For hedges that are already bare at the bottom, try reducing the height by a third and tapering the sides to let light in. Most species will gradually fill back in over one to two growing seasons.

## Hedge maintenance beyond trimming

Healthy hedges need more than just a haircut:

- **Feed in spring and autumn** with a balanced slow-release fertiliser to support dense growth
- **Water deeply during dry spells**, especially newly planted hedges and those in sandy soil
- **Mulch along the base** to retain moisture and suppress weeds
- **Watch for pests and diseases** — Lilly Pilly psyllid, scale insects, and fungal issues are common in Geelong hedges
