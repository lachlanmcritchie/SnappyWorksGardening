Mulching is one of the simplest things you can do to improve the health of your garden, yet it is often done at the wrong time or with the wrong material. For Geelong gardens dealing with dry summers, occasional frosts, and variable soil types, getting your mulch right makes a real difference. Here is everything you need to know.

## What does mulch actually do?

Before diving into timing and types, it helps to understand why mulch matters:

- **Retains soil moisture** by reducing evaporation — critical during Geelong's hot, dry summers
- **Suppresses weeds** by blocking sunlight from reaching weed seeds
- **Regulates soil temperature**, keeping roots cooler in summer and warmer in winter
- **Improves soil structure** as organic mulches break down over time
- **Reduces erosion** from heavy rain and wind
- **Gives garden beds a clean, finished appearance**

## When is the best time to mulch in Geelong?

### Late spring (October to November)

This is the ideal primary mulching window. The soil has warmed up, spring weeds have been removed, and you are laying down protection before the summer heat arrives. Mulching in late spring locks in soil moisture right when your plants need it most.

### Autumn (March to April)

A second application or top-up in autumn prepares your garden for winter. The mulch insulates plant roots against cold snaps and helps the soil retain the moisture from autumn rain.

### Times to avoid mulching

- **Mid-winter** when the soil is cold and waterlogged — mulch can trap too much moisture and encourage root rot
- **During extreme summer heat** — disturbing garden beds in 40-degree weather stresses plants further

## What type of mulch should you use?

Not all mulch is created equal. The right choice depends on what you are mulching and what you want to achieve.

### Organic mulches (break down over time)

- **Sugar cane mulch** — Affordable, easy to spread, breaks down within a season. Great for vegetable gardens and annual flower beds.
- **Pea straw** — Similar to sugar cane but slightly more nutrient-rich. Can contain pea seeds that sprout, though these are easily pulled or dug in.
- **Bark chips and pine bark** — Longer lasting, ideal for ornamental beds and around established trees and shrubs. Takes 2-3 years to break down.
- **Composted hardwood mulch** — A good all-rounder that improves soil as it decomposes. Works well in most Geelong garden beds.
- **Lucerne hay** — High in nitrogen, excellent for vegetable gardens. Breaks down quickly and feeds the soil.

### Inorganic mulches

- **Gravel and pebbles** — Permanent, low maintenance, suits native and drought-tolerant plantings. Does not improve soil but provides excellent drainage.
- **Recycled rubber** — Long lasting but does not benefit the soil. Best for play areas rather than garden beds.

### Which mulch for Geelong conditions?

For most Geelong gardens, **composted hardwood mulch or bark chips** strike the best balance between longevity, soil improvement, and moisture retention. If you have sandy soil (common in areas closer to the coast like Barwon Heads and Torquay), organic mulches that break down and add humus are especially valuable. For heavy clay soils found in parts of Corio, Norlane, and the northern suburbs, coarser mulches help improve drainage over time.

## How to mulch properly

Getting the technique right is just as important as choosing the right material.

1. **Weed the bed first.** Mulch suppresses new weeds but will not kill established ones. Remove all visible weeds, roots and all.
2. **Water the soil.** If the ground is dry, give it a good soak before mulching. You want to lock moisture in, not out.
3. **Spread evenly to 50-75mm depth.** Too thin and weeds push through. Too thick and water cannot penetrate.
4. **Keep mulch away from plant stems and tree trunks.** Leave a 50-100mm gap around the base of plants to prevent collar rot.
5. **Do not pile mulch into "volcanoes" around trees.** This common mistake traps moisture against bark and leads to decay.

## How often should you replace mulch?

This depends on the type:

- **Sugar cane and pea straw:** Every 3-6 months (they break down fast)
- **Composted hardwood:** Every 12-18 months
- **Bark chips:** Every 2-3 years
- **Gravel and pebbles:** Rarely, just top up as needed

A good rule of thumb is to check your mulch depth in spring and autumn. If it has thinned to less than 40mm, it is time for a top-up.

## Does mulch attract termites?

This is a common concern in Geelong. The short answer is that mulch does not attract termites to your property — termites are drawn to the soil beneath, not the mulch itself. However, keeping mulch away from building foundations (at least 300mm clearance) is sensible practice regardless.

## Does mulching help reduce weeding?

Absolutely. A properly applied layer of mulch can reduce weed growth by up to 90 percent. The key is maintaining adequate depth and topping up before the mulch thins out. For beds that are already weedy, a thorough hand-weed followed by fresh mulch is one of the most effective long-term solutions.
